You'd think ranks 6 through 10 would be the "lesser" films.

They're not.

*Train to Busan* introduced Korean cinema to the world. *Parasite* won the Academy Award for Best Picture. *Exhuma* went to Berlin. *Miracle in Cell No.7* was remade in three countries. And *The Roundup* brought Korea's post-COVID audiences back to theaters in the biggest way possible.

## #6 — Miracle in Cell No.7 (7번방의 선물, 2013) | 12.81 Million Admissions

![Miracle in Cell No.7 atmosphere image](/assets/img/posts/kmovie-cell-no7.jpg)
*A father with an intellectual disability. A daughter who won't give up. A cell block full of criminals with hearts of gold.*

### The One-Line Plot

Yong-gu, a man with an intellectual disability, is wrongly convicted of murder and thrown in prison. The hardened criminals in Cell No.7 take him in — and help smuggle in his young daughter, Ye-seung.

### Why Did 12 Million People See This?

**① Ryu Seung-ryong's performance is a career milestone**
Before this film, Ryu Seung-ryong was known mostly as a supporting comic actor. *Miracle in Cell No.7* changed everything. His portrayal of Yong-gu is tender, specific, and completely unsentimental — he never plays the disability for easy sympathy. It's one of the finest performances in Korean cinema, and it made audiences fall completely apart.

**② "An innocent person punished" is universal**
The story of a good, powerless person being crushed by a system that doesn't care — that resonates everywhere. You don't need to know anything about Korea to feel the rage and grief building through this film. And when it finally releases, it releases hard.

**③ It earns its tears by being funny first**
The criminals of Cell No.7 helping Yong-gu is played largely for comedy. It's absurd, warm, and surprisingly charming. That humor isn't filler — it's structural. The laughter in the first half makes the heartbreak in the second half land harder than it ever could on its own.

**④ Three countries remade it**
Turkey, Indonesia, and the Philippines all produced their own versions. The Turkish remake became the highest-grossing film in Turkish cinema history at the time. When a story works that well across that many cultures, it's not sentiment — it's a universal truth.

### The Scene You Can't Miss

**Ye-seung gets smuggled in.** The Cell No.7 crew hides Yong-gu's daughter in a backpack and sneaks her past the guards. It's ridiculous, tense, and impossibly sweet — and it captures exactly what makes this film tick. You'll be holding your breath and smiling at the same time.

## #7 — The Roundup (범죄도시2, 2022) | 12.69 Million Admissions

![The Roundup atmosphere image](/assets/img/posts/kmovie-roundup.jpg)
*Ma Dong-seok goes to Vietnam. The bad guys don't stand a chance.*

### The One-Line Plot

Detective Ma Seok-do tracks a Korean serial killer operating out of Vietnam. Jurisdiction is not a concern. Neither are the laws of physics.

### Why Did 12 Million People See This?

**① It was the first film people wanted to see in a theater after COVID**
*The Roundup* opened May 18, 2022. Audiences had been locked out of cinemas for two years. They came back for this — and they came back in massive numbers. It became the first Korean film to cross 10 million admissions in the post-pandemic era. That timing wasn't luck. The film delivered exactly what people needed: pure, uncomplicated, crowd-pleasing entertainment.

**② Ma Dong-seok is a genre unto himself**
After *Train to Busan* and *Crime City* (the first film), Ma Dong-seok's action style had become its own thing. Audiences don't just watch *The Roundup* for a good story. They watch it to see Ma Dong-seok punch someone very large, very hard, in very satisfying ways. The film knows this and commits to it completely.

**③ The sequel is better than the original**
*The Roundup* has a bigger location (Vietnam), a scarier villain, and tighter action set pieces than *Crime City* (2017). Sequels that outperform their predecessors are rare. This is one of them.

**④ It launched an unstoppable franchise**
*Crime City* (2017): 6.88M. *The Roundup* (2022): 12.69M. *The Roundup: No Way Out* (2023): 10.69M. *The Roundup: Punishment* (2024): 11.44M. All four films crossed major milestones. The series is now the most commercially dominant franchise in Korean cinema history.

### The Scene You Can't Miss

**The final confrontation.** Ma Seok-do vs. the main villain. No wire work. No fancy moves. Just Ma Dong-seok doing what Ma Dong-seok does — pushing forward like a freight train until the problem stops. The whole theater cheers. Every time.

## #8 — Exhuma (파묘, 2024) | 11.91 Million Admissions

![Exhuma atmosphere image](/assets/img/posts/kmovie-exhuma.jpg)
*A grave that should never have been opened. A film that stays with you for days.*

### The One-Line Plot

A wealthy Korean-American family hires a shaman and a geomancer to relocate an ancestral grave in Korea — hoping to break a generational curse. They should not have moved that grave.

### Why Did 11 Million People See This?

**① It went viral because the experience was too strong to keep quiet**
*Exhuma* had a quiet opening weekend. Then word started spreading. "You have to see this." "I couldn't sleep after." "Watch it in a group." Within two weeks, the theater lines were long. Horror films that spread through word-of-mouth are rare — it means the film delivers something genuinely hard to shake.

**② Korean shamanism as a horror system**
Western horror runs on ghosts, demons, and jump scares. *Exhuma* runs on *musok* — Korean shamanism — and the ancient belief that disturbing the dead has consequences. For international audiences, this is a completely unfamiliar set of rules, which makes it genuinely unpredictable. For Korean audiences, it taps into something cultural and ancestral that feels close to home.

**③ Director Jang Jae-hyun controls dread like a master**
Jang already proved himself with *The Priests* (2015) and *Svaha: The Sixth Finger* (2019). *Exhuma* is his biggest canvas yet. The first half is slow, oppressive, and methodical — building unease without releasing it. The second half pulls the trigger. The structure rewards patience with a payoff that hits genuinely hard.

**④ Four actors at the top of their game**
Choi Min-sik (of *Oldboy* fame) anchors the film as the geomancer. Kim Go-eun throws herself into the shaman role with total physical commitment. Yoo Hae-jin brings warmth and groundedness. Lee Do-hyun adds quiet tension. Each character has a clear function, and each actor delivers.

### The Scene You Can't Miss

**The first ritual.** Kim Go-eun's shaman performs a full *gut* (shamanic ceremony). The physical performance, the sound design, the lighting — it fills the screen with a kind of energy that makes your skin uncomfortable. You're not sure if you're watching something sacred or something dangerous. That ambiguity is the whole film.

## #9 — Train to Busan (부산행, 2016) | 11.57 Million Admissions

![Train to Busan atmosphere image](/assets/img/posts/kmovie-train-to-busan.jpg)
*Zombies on a bullet train. No exits. The film that made the world pay attention to Korean cinema.*

### The One-Line Plot

A father and his young daughter board the KTX from Seoul to Busan. The zombie apocalypse starts on the train. There is nowhere to go.

### Why Did 11 Million People See This?

**① The setup is a masterpiece of constraint**
Zombies in a contained space with no escape route — the premise does half the work. Every car door becomes a life-or-death decision. Every section of train becomes a different battleground. The geography of the train is used brilliantly from start to finish, and the tension never lets up across the full 118 minutes.

**② The emotional story is stronger than the horror**
Gong Yoo's character, Seok-woo, starts the film as a selfish, workaholic father. His daughter knows it. So do we. The film is really about whether he can become someone his daughter doesn't have to be ashamed of — and it earns every moment of his arc. *Train to Busan* makes you cry. That's not an accident. It's the point.

**③ Ma Dong-seok steals every scene he's in**
Gong Yoo is the lead, but Ma Dong-seok as Sang-hwa — a tough, loudmouthed man who is also the most genuinely decent person on the train — is what audiences remember. His tunnel sequence, punching through a wall of zombies with his bare hands, is legendary. It's where the Ma Dong-seok myth really begins.

**④ It put Korean genre cinema on the global map**
Before *Parasite*, *Train to Busan* was the film that made international audiences take Korean cinema seriously. It played at Cannes midnight screenings to a standing ovation. It was distributed in 167 countries. Hollywood talked about a remake. No Korean genre film had ever traveled that far, that fast.

### The Scene You Can't Miss

**The tunnel sequence.** The survivors have to move through a pitch-dark tunnel car packed with zombies. Ma Dong-seok leads. The camera barely lets you breathe. It's the most intense 90 seconds in Korean action cinema — and it launched a career.

## #10 — Parasite (기생충, 2019) | 10.31 Million Admissions

![Parasite atmosphere image](/assets/img/posts/kmovie-parasite.jpg)
*Best Picture. Best Director. Best Original Screenplay. Best International Feature. The night Korean cinema changed forever.*

### The One-Line Plot

The Kim family lives in a cramped semi-basement apartment. The Park family lives in a stunning architect-designed house. Through clever schemes, the Kims infiltrate the Parks' lives one by one. Then a basement door opens — and the film becomes something else entirely.

### Why Did 10 Million People See This?

**① The Oscars sent people back to theaters**
*Parasite* won four Academy Awards on February 9, 2020 — including Best Picture, the first time a non-English-language film had ever won. In Korea, it felt like a national moment. Audiences who had already seen it went back. Audiences who hadn't raced to catch up. The Oscar announcement was, functionally, the biggest marketing event in Korean film history.

**② The genre keeps shifting underneath you**
*Parasite* starts as a dark comedy. Then it becomes a thriller. Then it becomes a tragedy. Bong Joon-ho never signals the transitions — you realize what kind of film you're watching only after you're already deep inside it. That instability keeps you tense from the first frame to the last.

**③ The class commentary works everywhere**
"The wealthy can afford to be naive. The poor have to be clever." That's the engine of *Parasite* — and it's not a Korean idea. It's a universal one. Audiences in the US, UK, France, Japan, Brazil all recognized it as their own world. That's why the film crossed borders the way it did.

**④ The symbols reward rewatching**
Stairs going up, stairs going down. The smell that can't be washed away. The rain that floods a basement and fills a mansion's garden with water at the same time. Every visual choice means something. First-time viewers feel it. Repeat viewers understand it. That's a film designed to be seen more than once.

### The Scene You Can't Miss

**The basement door opens.** It divides the film in two. Before it: dark comedy and clever scheming. After it: something much more unsettling. Don't look it up. Don't let anyone tell you what's behind the door. Just let it happen — that moment of surprise is half of what makes *Parasite* work.

## 🔍 What These 5 Films Have in Common

**Emotion beats genre every time.**
*Train to Busan* is a zombie film that makes you cry about a father and daughter. *Miracle in Cell No.7* is a prison drama that makes you laugh before it breaks you. *Parasite* is a thriller that makes you think for days. The genre is the vehicle — emotion is always the destination.

**Bong Joon-ho has two films in the TOP 10.**
*The Host* at #4, *Parasite* at #10. Two completely different genres, two completely different tones, both in the all-time top tier. That's not a coincidence. That's what a great director looks like.

**Ma Dong-seok appears twice.**
*Train to Busan* (#9) and *The Roundup* (#7). At this point, his presence on a film's poster is essentially a box office guarantee.

**These films traveled.**
*Parasite* (4 Oscars), *Train to Busan* (Cannes, 167 countries), *Exhuma* (Berlin), *Miracle in Cell No.7* (remade 3 times). Rankings 6 through 10 didn't just do well in Korea — they changed how the world thinks about Korean cinema.
