## A Plan Twenty Years in the Making

If you have already watched *The Glory*, you know Moon Dong-eun's revenge was never about one big, dramatic moment. It was slow. It was patient. And that patience is exactly what made the ending so satisfying.

Let's break down how everything came together, and why this show hit so hard for viewers around the world.

## How Dong-eun's Plan Actually Worked

Dong-eun's strategy relied on placing herself close to her enemies and using their secrets, weaknesses, and distrust of one another against them. She became a homeroom teacher at the elementary school attended by Ye-sol, the daughter of her main bully, Park Yeon-jin. That move gave her a believable way to enter Yeon-jin's family life and pressure her through the person she cared about most.

From there, Dong-eun built a quiet network of allies. Joo Yeo-jeong, a doctor with his own painful past, became both her closest ally and the person who understood her most. Kang Hyeon-nam, a woman trapped in an abusive marriage, agreed to help Dong-eun gather information in exchange for a way to protect her own daughter.

Piece by piece, Dong-eun exposed the truth about her old bullies: Park Yeon-jin, Jeon Jae-jun, Lee Sa-ra, Choi Hye-jeong, and Son Myeong-o. Each of them had spent years hiding behind money and status. Dong-eun took that protection away one relationship, one secret, and one betrayal at a time.



## The Twist Nobody Saw Coming

One of the show's biggest surprises involves the death of Son Myeong-o. For most of the story, it looks like Park Yeon-jin killed him herself, and even Yeon-jin believes she is the one responsible.

The truth is more complicated. Yeon-jin did attack him, but it was actually Kim Gyeong-ran, a store employee who had also suffered under the same group of bullies years earlier, who delivered the final blow. For much of the story, Yeon-jin believes she killed Myeong-o and initially thinks the crime has been successfully covered up, unaware that someone else's hidden pain was tangled up with her own guilt the entire time.

This twist mattered because it showed something the show cared about deeply: bullying rarely stays contained to just one victim. Its damage spreads outward for years, quietly shaping the lives of people who were never even part of the original story.

## How the Ending Plays Out

By the final episodes, Dong-eun's plan reaches its target. Park Yeon-jin is eventually convicted of murder, both for killing Yoon So-hee years earlier and for Son Myeong-o's death, and is sent to prison. Her husband, Ha Do-yeong, learns the truth, divorces Yeon-jin, and moves to the United Kingdom with their daughter, Ye-sol, to protect her.

Here is the part that surprised many viewers: after fulfilling her revenge, Dong-eun plans to end her own life. It is not Yeo-jeong who stops her, but his mother, who pleads with Dong-eun to keep living, telling her that Yeo-jeong's own life depends on it. Months later, Dong-eun and Yeo-jeong reconcile, and the two choose to move forward together by turning their attention to Yeo-jeong's unfinished revenge against Kang Yeong-cheon, the man who murdered his father. Both take jobs at the prison where Yeong-cheon is being held, Dong-eun as a teacher and Yeo-jeong as a doctor.

That final choice turns *The Glory* from a simple revenge story into something a little more complicated. Dong-eun's story stops being only about closing one chapter of pain. It becomes about a new one just beginning.

## Symbols Worth Noticing

*The Glory* uses a few quiet visual details again and again:

- **Scars:** Dong-eun's physical scars, caused by a curling iron during the bullying, are a constant reminder that some wounds never fully disappear, even after revenge.
- **Fire and water:** Both elements show up repeatedly around key emotional turning points, often suggesting destruction paired with the possibility of cleansing or starting over.
- **The school itself:** The story keeps returning to school settings, framing them less as places of learning and more as spaces where cruelty was allowed to grow unchecked.

None of these symbols are explained out loud. The show trusts its audience to notice them on their own, which is part of why fans enjoyed picking the story apart online.

## Why the World Couldn't Stop Watching

*The Glory* did not just succeed in Korea. Part 2 was Netflix's most-viewed title worldwide during its debut week, pulling in 124.46 million hours viewed, and *The Glory* held the No. 1 spot on Netflix's global Non-English TV chart for three consecutive weeks. By late March 2023, it ranked No. 6 on Netflix's then-current all-time Most Popular Non-English TV list, with 413.05 million total hours viewed.

Domestically, the show also sparked real conversations about school violence. Kim Eun-sook has said she researched real Korean school-bullying incidents while writing the series and incorporated elements from some of those cases into the fictional story. That mix of fiction and real-world weight is part of why the show resonated so strongly, both inside Korea and far beyond it.

## Final Thoughts

*The Glory* works because it never treats revenge as simple or easy. Every step of Dong-eun's plan costs her something. By the time the credits roll, the show has made its point: surviving abuse and getting justice for it are two very different things, and healing from either one takes far longer than any single act of revenge ever could.
